3 Overall Questions and Motivations

  1. How has real estate prices for a Bostonian changed over time and how does that affordability share differ by neighborhood and property type?

    Motivation: I've felt the affordability gap in a very local way growing up in Lexington, MA, where price escalation and bidding wars are normal enough that it can start to feel "inevitable." Watching ordinary family homes sell at prices that would buy entirely different lifestyles elsewhere made me realize how quickly a place can become inaccessible to the people who actually want to live there long-term. For example, houses that are $500k in Dallas, Texas are sold for over $3 million in Lexington. The article I read for A1, Globe's "Towers of wealth," pushed that discomfort into the city context: if a growing slice of Boston's housing is priced for extremely high earners (or non-resident buyers), then "growth" can be happening in a way that doesn't translate into housing options for typical Bostonians. I want to quantify whether Boston's sales market is steadily moving the "affordable zone" out of reach, and whether that shift looks different in condos versus small multi-family, and across neighborhoods.

  2. What is the relationship between real estate development and real estate purchasing in Boston over time?

    Motivation: Boston's housing stock reflects a long arc from industrial growth to post-industrial wealth. The city's built form still carries the imprint of earlier eras-red-brick rowhouses, triple-deckers, and the iconic bay-window streetscapes associated with neighborhoods like the Back Bay. Yet, when I walk along the Charles it's obvious that the skyline is increasingly defined by glass high-rises and new luxury towers. That contrast raises a basic question about what "development" has meant in Boston over time: as the city shifted from a working port-and-manufacturing economy to a global center of finance, healthcare, higher education, and technology, did the dominant form of residential production shift with it? In particular, Boston's recent wave of condo construction suggests a move toward housing that is packaged, financed, and traded differently than traditional owner-occupied or rental forms. I want to understand how the composition of Boston's residential market has evolved-what gets built, who buys it, and how often properties turn over-and why condos, especially in newer buildings, appear to occupy such a central role.

  3. Are the "high-price clusters" actually driven by a small number of new condo developments in recent years?

    Motivation: "Tower of Wealth" describes how Boston has tight pockets of very high prices which are large condominium projects entering the market as discrete events. Hundreds of units delivered at once, many transactions recorded in a short window, and price points set near the top of the distribution. This raises the possibility that what looks like a broad "luxury cluster" may actually be the footprint of a few new towers rather than a diffuse transformation of surrounding housing. To distinguish between these mechanisms, I want to move from neighborhood averages to building-level purchase histories, tracking how units change hands over time within the same development. Examining unit-level histories also opens a question about the nature of demand in these towers. If early buyers frequently resell within short holding periods, then these buildings may function partly as financial assets rather than conventional housing. Conversely, longer holding periods and predominantly individual ownership would suggest more stable, resident-driven demand.

Summary

Main Lessons Learned

This analysis shows that Boston's residential market has become more expensive while becoming less active in total transaction volume. In the overview, sales counts peak around 2000-2004 and then decline, but median prices and price spread continue rising for most of the period. That divergence matters: a market can look "strong" by price growth while still becoming less accessible if fewer households can transact. Spatially, transactions are heavily concentrated in condo-dominant central neighborhoods, and several high-density purchase nodes appear to reflect large multi-unit developments rather than diffuse neighborhood-wide shifts.

When development is linked to purchasing, the story is not a single continuous trend. Boston shows an older, high-turnover legacy housing stock (especially late-1800s to early-1900s build eras) and a newer post-2000 segment with a stronger price premium. In other words, old and new stock can coexist in one market but behave differently in both turnover and price formation. Building-level checks of high-price clusters reinforce this point: concentrated tower projects can create large local transaction bursts that shape citywide averages and narratives.

The practical conclusion is that "affordability" cannot be read from one metric alone. Counts, medians, dispersion, property-type composition, and location all need to be read together. A shrinking transaction base plus rising medians can indicate stronger exclusionary pressure, especially when activity is concentrated in condominium developments targeted at higher purchasing power.

Technical Lessons (Tableau)

A major technical lesson was that worksheet design decisions strongly affect interpretation. I repeatedly switched between raw counts and share-based views, linear and log scales, and citywide versus filtered maps. This made it clear that no single chart form can carry the full analytical argument.

Calculated fields were essential for cleaning and structuring the analysis. Useful patterns to filter in Tableau's Create Calculated Field included:

  • Data cleaning and filtering: IF [Latitude] != 0 AND [Longitude] != 0 THEN [Price] END to remove invalid geocodes.
  • Null safety and robust logic: IFNULL([Field], 0), ZN([Measure]), and explicit CASE/IF branches to avoid silent errors.

Example: Question 3.5 (Repeat-Sale Overlay / Dumbbell)

For the last graph, I used a repeat-sale overlay approach in Tableau. The key assumption is that Address identifies the unit (or at least the entity being tracked). I created LOD fields for first/last sale dates and first/last sale prices:

Date: 

{ FIXED [Address] : MIN([Prsldate]) }
{ FIXED [Address] : MAX([Prsldate]) }

Price: 

{ FIXED [Address] :
    MIN(
        IF [Prsldate] = { FIXED [Address] : MIN([Prsldate]) }
        THEN [Price]
        END
    )
}
{ FIXED [Address] :
    MAX(
        IF [Prsldate] = { FIXED [Address] : MAX([Prsldate]) }
        THEN [Price]
        END
    )
}

I also filtered to repeat-sales only so first and last are meaningful. One approach is a boolean LOD filter: 

{ FIXED [Address] : COUNT([Prsldate]) } >= 2

Practical constraints: this becomes unreadable with too many addresses, so it works best after filtering to a building or Top N by price span. I also used tooltips with first/last sale date and holding-period context. If Address is building-level (not unit-level), a building-level median first-year vs median last-year version is more appropriate than unit-level overlays.

Another technical lesson was quality control: unusual visual breaks (like the post-2021 behavior) are not automatically "real trends." They can reveal coverage problems or type-specific missingness. Cross-checking multiple worksheets before interpreting a pattern prevented overclaiming.

Subtheme: Speculation (Insights and Implications)

Under the subtheme Speculation, the evidence suggests that part of Boston's high-price landscape is produced by asset-like dynamics concentrated in specific condo developments. High-price clusters are often tied to discrete project launches with many unit sales in short windows, and these events can dominate local market signals. This means some apparent neighborhood-level "luxury transformation" may actually be the footprint of a few buildings entering and recirculating through the market.

The implications are important. If price growth is increasingly anchored by condo-heavy, high-value developments while overall transaction participation declines, then market access may shift toward households with higher income or wealth buffers. That does not prove every buyer is speculative, but it does show a structure where speculative or investment-oriented behavior can have outsized influence on observed prices and affordability thresholds.

For policy and future research, this points to the need for building-level monitoring in addition to neighborhood averages. Useful next checks include ownership type, holding period, repeat-sale frequency, and occupancy patterns in high-density condo projects. These indicators would help distinguish resident-driven demand from capital-driven turnover and clarify when "growth" expands housing access versus when it primarily reinforces exclusion at the top of the distribution.
